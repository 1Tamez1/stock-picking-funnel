## 1. Stock Candidate Business Underwriting Questionnaire v2
BODY:
Purpose: decide whether the business itself is strong enough, understandable enough, and durable enough to justify continuation to **Management Underwriting**, **Financial Underwriting**, and **Valuation and Position Size**.

This is the second stage of the funnel:

1. Screening
2. Business Underwriting
3. Management Underwriting
4. Financial Underwriting
5. Valuation and Position Size
6. Execution Rules

This document is only for **Business Underwriting**. It is not a management memo, not a balance-sheet memo, not a valuation memo, and not a buy decision. Its job is to prove or disprove the business itself: market boundary, customer value proposition, value chain, profit-pool position, source of competitive advantage, operating statistics, unit economics, capital intensity, reinvestment logic, capital-cycle risk, and competitive destruction risk.

This document is designed to be used **after Screening**, not instead of Screening. It should deepen the passed business claims, not repeat the stage-1 screen.

## 2. Core Rules
BODY:
- **Pass Business Underwriting** means "continue deeper underwriting," not "buy."
- **Watchlist** means "the business may be interesting, but one or more business claims remain unproven."
- **Archive** means "stop unless the business, evidence, or price changes materially."
- Start from the **screening handoff**, not from a blank sheet.
- Use the same evidence grades and confidence tags as the Screening Questionnaire.
- Do not re-answer stage-1 questions unless Business Underwriting changes the answer materially.
- Business Underwriting answers business questions only: why customers buy, why they stay, where the profits sit, why competitors fail, what capital the business really needs, and how the economics break.
- Management claims are not business proof. Use competitor, customer, supplier, channel, trade, and operating evidence whenever possible.
- Define the market boundary narrowly enough to make competition real.
- A moat is not a label. Tie every claimed advantage to a mechanism: **supply advantage**, **demand advantage / customer captivity**, **scale advantage**, **regulatory protection**, **network standard**, or **local dominance**.
- Distinguish a true **franchise** from a good period, a shortage, a favorable cycle, or a well-marketed commodity.
- Distinguish **economic goodwill** from accounting goodwill and from vague brand affection.
- Growth is not automatically good. Obvious market growth does not guarantee attractive profits.
- High returns attract capital. Ask what happens if capacity expands, channels shift, or rivals copy.
- If the business cannot be understood after proper segmentation, competitor work, and field evidence, classify it **Too Hard**.
- Preserve downstream questions for later stages; do not let later stages solve a business problem that should be solved here.
- Do not use a score. Scores create false comparability across unlike businesses.
- The final output must state the next action in the funnel.

## 12. Part IV. Source Of Competitive Advantage
BODY:
Judge the business by mechanism, not by vocabulary.

## 13. 1. Supply Advantage
BODY:
Questions:

- Does the company produce or deliver at lower cost than relevant rivals?
- If yes, why: location, procurement, know-how, process, utilization, asset base, route density, or experience curve?
- Is the advantage durable, or can competitors match it with time and capital?
- Is the cost advantage local, segment-specific, or enterprise-wide?
- What operating evidence supports the claim?

Rule:

- **Pass** if a real and defensible cost or supply advantage is evidenced.
- **Watchlist** if the advantage may exist but the mechanism is still unproven.
- **Archive** if the supposed cost advantage is just temporary underutilization elsewhere, favorable cycle, or unsupported management language.

**Result**: Pass / Watchlist / Archive / Not Applicable

## 14. 2. Demand Advantage / Customer Captivity
BODY:
Questions:

- Are customers captive because of habit, switching costs, search costs, installed base, workflow embedding, regulation, qualification, or trust?
- Is the product needed or desired strongly enough that customers resist substitutes?
- Are renewal, repeat purchase, retention, replacement cycle, or attach rates observable?
- Is customer captivity strongest in existing customers, new customers, or both?
- What evidence shows customers pay for value rather than buy only on price?

Rule:

- **Pass** if customer captivity or demand advantage is real and observable.
- **Watchlist** if customer captivity may exist but the evidence is thin.
- **Archive** if the customer relationship is shallow and price-led.
