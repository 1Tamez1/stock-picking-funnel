# Part II. Screening Snapshot

## 1. Business In Plain English

- What does the company sell?

**Answer**: NIKE designs, markets, and sells athletic footwear, apparel, equipment, accessories, and services worldwide under the NIKE Brand, Jordan Brand, and Converse [F, S1; High].

- Who pays, why do they pay, and how often?

**Answer**: Consumers ultimately pay for athletic performance, sport-inspired lifestyle, brand identity, comfort, and fashion. Sales occur through wholesale partners and NIKE Direct stores / digital channels. Purchase frequency depends on category: footwear and apparel can be recurring, but not contractual [F/I, S1; High].

- What are the main revenue drivers?

**Answer**: Global unit demand, average selling prices, product heat, category mix, channel mix between wholesale and direct, regional growth, launch cadence, athlete / team associations, and foreign exchange [F/I, S1/S2; High].

- What are the main cost drivers?

**Answer**: Third-party manufacturing costs, freight, tariffs, inventory obsolescence / markdowns, demand creation expense, endorsement contracts, digital / retail operating overhead, wages, leases, and FX [F, S1/S2; High].

- What capital is required to maintain the business?

**Answer**: Physical capital needs are modest relative to sales: recent annual capex has usually been well below 2% of revenue. The more important maintenance spend is demand creation, athlete endorsement, product design, talent, and marketplace investment, which is expensed through the income statement [F/I, S1/S2; High].

- What capital is required to grow the business?

**Answer**: Growth requires marketing / endorsement spend, product creation, working capital for inventory and receivables, selective retail and digital investment, and distribution / supply-chain capacity. It is not factory-capital heavy because manufacturing is largely outsourced [F/I, S1; High].

- What are the two or three variables that matter most to value?

**Answer**: (1) Whether the NIKE / Jordan brand moat still supports full-price demand and pricing; (2) normalized gross and operating margins after the marketplace reset; (3) free cash flow conversion and capital allocation discipline [I; High].

- What important part of the business is still outside my competence?

**Answer**: Real-time brand heat by category and geography, especially running, basketball, lifestyle franchises, China, and youth consumer preference. This requires customer / channel checks beyond filings [V; High].

**Result**: Needs Verification

## 2. Why Is This Stock On The Desk?

- Why is this candidate being screened now?

**Answer**: The share price has fallen materially from prior highs while NIKE still has a global brand, high historical returns on capital, and a strong balance sheet. The question is whether the stock has moved from "expensive quality" to "potentially reasonable former compounder," or whether the market is correctly pricing structural deterioration [I; Medium].

- Is the attraction quality, price, temporary controversy, insider behavior, portfolio fit, or something else?

**Answer**: Mostly quality plus lower price. The controversy is operational: revenue decline, gross-margin compression, China weakness, Direct channel pressure, Converse weakness, tariffs, and a new CEO / reset [F/I, S2/S3; High].

- What is the simplest version of the potential thesis?

**Answer**: NIKE remains one of the world's best athletic brands, current earnings are depressed by a fixable marketplace / product reset, and a strong balance sheet allows the company to recover without permanent capital impairment. If normalized owner earnings recover to roughly $4.5-5.0 billion, the current price could be reasonable [I; Medium].

- What is the simplest reason to reject it?

**Answer**: The brand moat may be weaker than the historical financials imply. If discounting, traffic weakness, and competition from adidas, On, Hoka, Lululemon, local China brands, and fashion cycles are structural, normalized earnings are much lower and the stock is not cheap [O/I, S2/S5; Medium].

- What would make this a false positive at the screening stage?

**Answer**: Treating "NIKE is a great brand" as proof of current pricing power, while recent financials show gross margin down, cash conversion weak, and management still resetting the marketplace [F/I, S2/S3; High].

# Part III. Hard Screening Gates

## 1. Circle Of Competence And Understandability

**Result**: Pass

**Notes**: The business model is understandable: design / brand / distribution economics with outsourced manufacturing. The hard part is not conceptual complexity but consumer preference durability. That gap is named and verifiable through Business Underwriting [F/I; High].

## 2. Operating History And Evidence Base

**Result**: Pass

**Notes**: NIKE has a long public operating record and remained profitable through multiple cycles. FY2021-FY2025 revenue ranged from $44.5 billion to $51.4 billion before falling to $46.3 billion in FY2025; the company remained profitable each year [F, S1; High]. Current business is materially similar, though Direct / wholesale mix and product portfolio reset are important changes [F/I, S2/S3; Medium].

## 3. Business Quality And Moat Plausibility

**Result**: Watchlist

**Notes**: Plausible moat: brand, scale, athlete relationships, product creation, distribution, and global consumer awareness. Evidence includes large revenue base, historically high gross margins, very high returns on invested capital, and external brand-strength evidence [F/O, S1/S7; Medium]. The watchlist issue is that recent financials also show moat pressure: FY2025 revenue down 10%, gross margin down 190 bps to 42.7%, and Q3 FY2026 gross margin down to 40.2% [F, S1/S3; High]. adidas reported record 2025 revenue, 13% currency-neutral adidas-brand growth, and 51.6% gross margin, which is relevant competitive evidence that Nike's weakness is not purely industry-wide [F/O, S5; Medium].

## 4. No Turnaround Or Severe-Change Dependence

**Result**: Watchlist

**Notes**: The core company is profitable and financially strong, so this is not a distress turnaround. But the investment case at today's price likely needs margin recovery and proof that the product / marketplace reset is working. That dependence is too important to pass screening today [F/I, S2/S3; High].

## 5. Integrity, Accounting, And Incentive Kill Risk

**Result**: Pass

**Notes**: No material accounting or integrity kill issue found in the quick review. The FY2025 10-K includes an unqualified audit opinion and internal-control opinion [F, S1; High]. Compensation is heavily incentive-based, with a large long-term equity component and CEO target annual long-term incentive mix of PSUs, options, and RSUs [F, S4; Medium]. The main concern is capital allocation, not integrity: NIKE bought back shares under the current $18 billion authorization at an average price of $97.57, while the current share price is around $45-46; repurchases were paused during FY2026 Q1 [F/I, S2; High].

## 6. Balance Sheet And Permanent-Loss Risk

**Result**: Pass

**Notes**: Balance sheet is strong. At February 28, 2026, cash and short-term investments were about $8.06 billion, debt was about $8.03 billion, no commercial paper was outstanding, and the company stated it was in compliance with facilities and had sufficient liquidity for the next twelve months and beyond [F, S2; High]. Lease liabilities are real, but not a distress risk at current scale [F/I, S2; High].

## 7. Valuation Knowability And Apparent Margin Of Safety

**Result**: Watchlist

**Notes**: Value is roughly estimable, but the estimate depends on normalized owner earnings. Current TTM net income is only about $2.25 billion and TTM free cash flow is about $1.05 billion after capex, both depressed [F/I, S1/S2; Medium]. If normalized owner earnings recover to $4.0-5.0 billion, valuation may be reasonable. If current economics are closer to the new normal, the stock is expensive [I; Medium].

## 8. Opportunity Cost And Funnel Fit

**Result**: Watchlist

**Notes**: This fits the normal operating-business funnel, but scarce underwriting time should not be spent until the trigger is clear: either a lower price with no further moat evidence deterioration, or evidence that normalized owner earnings can recover without heroic assumptions [I; Medium].

## Moat Hypothesis

- What is the moat? Do not name a moat type without evidence.

**Answer**: NIKE's moat hypothesis is a combination of global brand trust / aspiration, athlete and team relationships, product-creation scale, wholesale shelf access, direct consumer reach, and marketing capability. The moat is not simply the Swoosh; it must show up in full-price demand, gross margin, inventory health, and share stability [F/O/I; Medium].

- What observed facts support the moat?

**Answer**: NIKE generated $46.3 billion of FY2025 revenue despite a bad year, remained profitable, and historically produced high returns on capital with modest physical capital needs [F, S1; High]. Brand Finance ranked Nike as the strongest apparel brand globally in 2025, with a high brand strength score and evidence of price acceptance in several markets [O, S7; Medium]. Historical FY2021-FY2024 operating margins were roughly 12-16%, before falling sharply in FY2025/TTM [F/I, S1/S2; High].

- Is the moat widening, stable, narrowing, or unproven?

**Answer**: Stable over a long historical view, but currently under pressure / possibly narrowing. The recent financial evidence is too negative to call the moat widening [I; Medium].

- How would a well-funded competitor attack it?

**Answer**: Attack product speed and fashion relevance in running / lifestyle; win wholesale mindshare; undercut Nike in value channels; sponsor younger athletes and creators; localize product in China; use social commerce and limited drops to capture youth attention [O/I; Medium].

- What evidence would prove the moat is weaker than believed?

**Answer**: Sustained market-share loss, continued gross margin below historical range after inventory normalization, rising promotions, weak full-price sell-through, China / Converse weakness persisting, and competitor growth continuing while NIKE remains flat or declining [F/O/I; High].

- What must Business Underwriting prove next about the moat?

**Answer**: That the brand still drives full-price demand and category relevance in the specific categories that matter, not merely that NIKE was historically powerful [V; High].

**Business Quality Result**: Adequate
