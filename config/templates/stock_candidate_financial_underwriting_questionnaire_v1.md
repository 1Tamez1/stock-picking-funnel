# Stock Candidate Financial Underwriting Questionnaire v1

Purpose: decide whether a stock deserves entry into **Valuation and Position Size**, belongs on a watchlist with a defined financial trigger, should be returned to an earlier underwriting stage, or should be archived.

This is the fourth stage of the funnel:

1. Screening
2. Business Underwriting
3. Management Underwriting
4. Financial Underwriting
5. Valuation and Position Size
6. Execution Rules

This document is only for **Financial Underwriting**. It is not a business memo, not a management memo, not a valuation memo, and not a buy decision. Its job is to turn reported numbers into owner economics, test whether the accounting describes reality, stress the balance sheet, and decide whether the financial record is clean enough to value.

This document is designed to be used **after Management Underwriting**, not instead of Management Underwriting. It should deepen the accounting, cash-flow, capital-structure, and normalization questions left open by earlier stages. It should not repeat market boundary, moat mechanism, culture, or capital-allocation intent unless the numbers materially change those conclusions.

## Core Rules

- **Pass Financial Underwriting** means "open Valuation and Position Size," not "buy."
- **Watchlist** means "the numbers may be good enough later, but one or two specific financial facts or conditions are missing."
- **Archive** means "weak economics, aggressive accounting, or financial fragility create unacceptable permanent-loss risk."
- **Return** means "the apparent financial problem is really a business-quality or management-quality problem and should go back to the correct stage."
- Start from the **Management Underwriting handoff**, not from a blank sheet.
- Do not re-answer stage-1, stage-2, or stage-3 questions unless financial work materially changes the answer.
- Financial Underwriting answers financial questions only: statement reliability, owner earnings, return on capital, working capital, capital structure, dilution math, normalization, and readiness for valuation.
- Use owner earnings, not reported EPS, adjusted EBITDA, or promotional non-GAAP metrics, as the center of the analysis.
- Start with the cash flow statement and balance sheet, then use the income statement as a cross-check.
- Evaluate per-share outcomes, not aggregate growth.
- High returns created by leverage, tax quirks, or accounting cosmetics are not high returns.
- A strong income statement cannot rescue a weak balance sheet.
- A low multiple cannot rescue dishonest accounting, chronic dilution, or value-destructive reinvestment.
- Normalize with evidence. Do not treat temporary working-capital releases, peak margins, reserve releases, or refinancing assumptions as durable economics.
- When a mature business or full cycle history exists, prefer 10-year trend work to 3-5-year snapshots.
- Thresholds are guides, not automatic rules. The burden of proof rises when the business is cyclical, opaque, leveraged, acquisitive, or balance-sheet-sensitive.
- For banks, insurers, and other financials, use the financial/insurer sections and do not force industrial metrics where they do not belong.
- Do not reopen moat, compensation, or acquisition-strategy debates except where those issues show up numerically in returns, dilution, cash generation, or capital structure.
- If the thesis works only on adjusted EBITDA while depreciation, capex, working capital, reserves, or dilution are real, stop.
- Unknowns are not neutral. A major unknown is a reason to Watchlist, Return, or Archive.

Target depth:

- **Archive**: often 45-90 minutes after building a simple financial map.
- **Watchlist**: usually 2-4 hours.
- **Pass Financial Underwriting**: enough work to support valuation, not enough to set a buy price or position size.

# Evidence, Sources, And Confidence

Use evidence grades beside important claims.

- **F** = audited, reported, or directly filed fact
- **O** = observable operating, customer, competitive, or industry evidence
- **M** = management claim
- **I** = analyst inference
- **V** = item to verify later

Use confidence tags beside important judgments.

- **High** = supported by several reliable facts or a long relevant record
- **Medium** = plausible, but one important item needs more evidence
- **Low** = early read, thin evidence, or material uncertainty

Rule: if a financial underwriting decision depends mainly on **M**, **I**, or **V**, the stock normally belongs on the Watchlist or should be returned, not Pass Financial Underwriting.

Minimum source expectations for **Pass Financial Underwriting**:

- Latest annual report or 10-K
- Latest quarterly report or 10-Q
- At least 10 years of income statement, balance sheet, and cash flow history when available, and a full-cycle history when the business is cyclical
- Debt maturity, lease, pension, reserve, tax, and stock-compensation notes
- Share-count history and capital-allocation history
- Working-capital detail, including receivables, inventory, payables, contract assets, and deferred revenue where relevant
- Non-GAAP reconciliations, if management uses adjusted metrics
- Capitalization-policy review, including software, R&D, content, implementation, cloud, or customer-acquisition cost treatment where relevant
- Acquisition / intangible / purchase-accounting review if the company is acquisitive
- For financials and insurers: regulatory capital, liquidity, funding, reserve or credit-quality, and asset-liability disclosures

If these are not reviewed, Pass Financial Underwriting requires an explicit reason.

# Part IV. Hard Financial Gates

Each hard gate should result in **Pass**, **Watchlist**, or **Archive**. A Watchlist result is acceptable only if the uncertainty is specific and verifiable. If the issue clearly belongs to Business or Management Underwriting, note that in the gate and use **Return** in the final decision.

## 1. Accounting Reality And Statement Cleanliness

Questions:

- Do reported results reconcile across the income statement, cash flow statement, and balance sheet?
- Are non-GAAP adjustments narrow, credible, and truly non-recurring?
- Are contract assets, unbilled receivables, deferred revenue, and channel inventory consistent with reported revenue quality?
- Are revenue recognition, capitalization policies, reserves, warranties, and impairment decisions conservative enough?
- Are capitalized R&D, software, cloud, content, implementation, or customer-acquisition costs sensible, or are operating expenses being pushed into assets?
- Are depreciation, amortization, and maintenance-capex assumptions economically sensible?
- Are tax rates, pension assumptions, and deferred items understandable and not doing hidden earnings work?
- Are lease, supplier-finance, factoring, securitization, or other financing arrangements being treated as financing rather than disguised operations?
- Is stock-based compensation treated as a real cost?
- Are internal-control, audit, restatement, or segment-reporting concerns absent?
- Does management rely more on owner economics or on EBITDA and adjusted narratives?

Hard-fail items:

- Material restatement or internal-control concern
- Recurring "one-time" charges that look structural
- Contract assets, receivables, or channel inventory trends that make revenue quality suspect
- Capitalization abuse or reserve-release dependence
- Capitalized operating costs that materially flatter current economics
- Opaque acquisition accounting or promotional add-backs
- Refusal to expense real economic costs
- Auditor, disclosure, or segment opacity that makes economics unknowable

Rule:

- **Pass** if the statements appear to describe economic reality plainly enough to underwrite.
- **Watchlist** if one narrow accounting item needs verification but the rest of the record is clean.
- **Archive** if aggressive accounting, opacity, or weak controls materially undermine trust in the numbers.

**Result**: Pass / Watchlist / Archive

**Notes**:

## 2. Owner Earnings And Cash Conversion

Questions:

- Can owner earnings be estimated conservatively from reported numbers?
- Are maintenance capex and required working-capital needs understood rather than assumed away?
- Do cash from operations, free cash flow, and owner earnings broadly support the earnings story over a cycle?
- Are lease, pension, warranty, restructuring, legal, tax, or reserve costs treated as real recurring economics where appropriate?
- Is the business usually owner-earnings positive in normal years?
- Are cash conversion swings explainable by the business model, or are they warning signs?

Rule:

- **Pass** if owner earnings are reasonably estimable and cash conversion supports the business story.
- **Watchlist** if one important item such as maintenance capex, working capital, or cash taxes still needs work.
- **Archive** if reported earnings materially overstate owner economics or cash conversion is structurally weak without a convincing reason.

**Result**: Pass / Watchlist / Archive

**Notes**:

## 3. Returns On Capital And Margin Quality

Questions:

- Are ROIC, ROE, and return on tangible capital attractive without undue leverage?
- Are returns stable enough through the cycle to be economically meaningful?
- Are returns on incremental capital acceptable?
- Are gross, operating, and free-cash-flow margins resilient enough for the business type?
- Are the returns real business economics, or mostly the product of leverage, tax quirks, temporary mix, or accounting treatment?
- If returns are mediocre, is that because the business is poor, temporarily depressed, or heavily reinvesting at good prospective returns?

Rule:

- **Pass** if returns and margins are good enough, real enough, and stable enough to support valuation.
- **Watchlist** if the current level is depressed or noisy but the normalization case is specific and testable.
- **Archive** if economics are persistently weak, leverage-dependent, or too unstable to anchor valuation.

**Result**: Pass / Watchlist / Archive

**Notes**:

## 4. Balance Sheet, Liquidity, And Permanent-Loss Risk

Questions:

- Can the company survive a recession, credit freeze, or operating shock without permanent owner impairment?
- Are debt maturities, covenants, refinancing needs, or floating-rate exposures manageable?
- What is the fixed-versus-floating, secured-versus-unsecured, and short-term-versus-term funding mix?
- Are leases, pensions, legal liabilities, guarantees, supplier finance, environmental liabilities, or other obligations material?
- Is reported liquidity real, or is cash trapped, pledged, or operationally unavailable?
- Are derivatives, contingent liabilities, or off-balance-sheet vehicles material and understandable?
- Is leverage a tool, or is it the main reason the economics look acceptable?
- For financials and insurers, are regulatory capital headroom, liquidity, asset quality, reserves, underwriting discipline, and asset-liability matching understandable?

Rule:

- **Pass** if the balance sheet is conservative enough for the business type and downside case.
- **Watchlist** if leverage is probably manageable but one stress, covenant, reserve, or funding issue still needs resolution.
- **Archive** if debt, liquidity, hidden liabilities, reserve risk, or asset quality can create permanent impairment.

**Result**: Pass / Watchlist / Archive

**Notes**:

## 5. Per-Share Economics, Dilution, And Retained-Capital Translation

Use the numerical record here. Intent, biography, and governance design belong primarily to Management Underwriting unless the numbers force a different conclusion.

Questions:

- Have revenue, earnings, owner earnings, and value grown per share, not just in aggregate?
- Has share count been stable, shrinking, or rising?
- Is stock-based compensation modest and sensibly structured, or is it a recurring owner cost and dilution machine?
- Have buybacks occurred at sensible prices?
- Have acquisitions improved per-share value, or mainly increased size and adjusted EBITDA?
- Has retained capital likely created at least one dollar of per-share value for each dollar retained over a reasonable period?

Rule:

- **Pass** if per-share economics are favorable and retained capital appears value-creating.
- **Watchlist** if the picture is mixed but one specific capital-allocation question can still be resolved.
- **Archive** if dilution, buybacks, acquisitions, or retained-capital results are clearly owner-destructive.

**Result**: Pass / Watchlist / Archive

**Notes**:

## 6. Cyclicality, Normalization, And Forecast Reliability

Questions:

- Are current earnings, margins, credit losses, or working-capital flows above, below, or near normal?
- What must normalize, and what should be treated as structurally changed?
- Are volume, price, mix, input cost, credit, reserve, and utilization assumptions explicit?
- Can a low, base, and high owner-earnings range be estimated without heroic assumptions?
- Does the normalization case depend on mean reversion, management promises, refinancing, or favorable macro?
- What would make the normalized range wrong?

Rule:

- **Pass** if normalized economics can be estimated conservatively enough to support valuation.
- **Watchlist** if one or two specific normalization items still need evidence.
- **Archive** if economics are too cyclical, too opaque, or too dependent on heroic recovery assumptions to value responsibly.

**Result**: Pass / Watchlist / Archive

**Notes**:

## 7. Business-Type Fit And Specialist Financial Standard

Questions:

- Is this best analyzed as an asset-light compounder, a good predictable business, an asset-heavy business, a cyclical, a financial/insurer, a roll-up, or a special structure?
- Are the chosen financial metrics appropriate to that business type?
- For financials and insurers, are capital, funding, reserves or credit quality, and asset-liability risks understandable enough to underwrite?
- For roll-ups or serial acquirers, are acquired economics separated from organic economics?
- For asset-heavy businesses, is replacement capex and inflation reality reflected?
- For negative-working-capital or float businesses, is the financing advantage durable and owner-friendly?

Rule:

- **Pass** if the correct financial lens is being used and the key specialist risks are understandable enough.
- **Watchlist** if one specialist item still needs verification.
- **Archive** if the wrong lens is required to make the business look acceptable, or if specialist risks make value unknowable.

**Result**: Pass / Watchlist / Archive

**Notes**:

## 8. Ready For Valuation And Position Size

Questions:

- Can valuation be anchored on normalized owner earnings, asset value, spread earnings, or another defensible measure?
- Is financial uncertainty narrow enough to set a reasonable value range later?
- Can Valuation and Position Size use a sensible reinvestment-rate and incremental-return assumption, or is that still guesswork?
- What can valuation safely assume, and what must it refuse to assume?
- Is the remaining debate mainly about price and required return, not accounting cleanliness or survivability?
- If the company is not ready for valuation, what single missing financial fact matters most?

Rule:

- **Pass** if the financial record is clean enough and the normalized economics are knowable enough to value.
- **Watchlist** if one specific financial fact or condition still blocks valuation.
- **Archive** if financial uncertainty is too large for valuation to be more than guesswork.

**Result**: Pass / Watchlist / Archive

# Part VIII. What Must Be True / What To Verify

This replaces broad "financial assumptions." Do not forecast for the sake of forecasting. List the few financial things that must be true for the stock to deserve Valuation and Position Size.

Good examples:

- Reported earnings are reasonably close to owner earnings.
- Maintenance capex is not materially above depreciation over time.
- Working-capital releases are not being mistaken for durable cash generation.
- Returns on capital are not leverage-driven.
- Stock compensation is a manageable owner cost, not a hidden transfer.
- The balance sheet can survive a bad year without forced dilution.
- Retained capital is still creating per-share value.
- Current margins are below normal for temporary rather than structural reasons.

Use no more than seven items. If the thesis needs more than seven major financial things to be true, the economics are probably too fragile.

# Part X. Financial Pattern And Next Valuation Standard

Pick the category based on the economics, not on the story.

## Category Options

### Asset-Light Compounder

Traits:

- High returns on capital without much leverage
- Strong owner-earnings conversion
- Modest maintenance capex
- Limited dilution
- Durable reinvestment or cash-distribution flexibility

Financial standard:

- Can pass with a narrower margin of financial error only if accounting is clean and incremental returns are real.

Valuation must respect:

- Do not understate stock compensation, reinvestment needs, or normalization risk just because the business is high quality.

### Good Predictable Business

Traits:

- Acceptable returns on capital
- Reasonable cash conversion
- Manageable leverage
- Moderate reinvestment needs
- Per-share economics are sound but not extraordinary

Financial standard:

- Needs stable owner earnings, a survivable balance sheet, and no major accounting concern.

Valuation must respect:

- Demand a real return from the current price; do not pay compounder multiples for merely decent economics.

### Asset-Heavy / Reinvestment-Heavy Business

Traits:

- High ongoing capex
- Material depreciation and replacement needs
- Inflation and capacity matter
- Cash conversion can differ materially from accounting earnings

Financial standard:

- Pass only if maintenance capex, replacement economics, and normalized returns are understood.

Valuation must respect:

- Use owner earnings after real reinvestment needs, not EBITDA or headline EPS.
- Treat capex-to-depreciation, working-capital drag, and asset replacement as economics, not footnotes.

### Cyclical / Commodity / Balance-Sheet-Sensitive Business

Traits:

- Margins and returns move with the cycle
- Working capital swings are material
- Downside survivability matters heavily
- Peak earnings are dangerous

Financial standard:

- Pass only if normalized or trough-aware economics and downside balance-sheet resilience are credible.

Valuation must respect:

- Do not capitalize peak margins or temporary cash releases.

### Financial / Insurer

Traits:

- Value depends on underwriting, credit quality, reserves, capital, funding, liquidity, and asset-liability management

Financial standard:

- Pass only if capital, reserving or credit quality, funding, and asset quality are understandable enough to underwrite.

Valuation must respect:

- Normalize leverage, credit costs, and regulatory-capital headroom; do not rely on headline ROE or industrial shortcuts.

### Roll-Up / Serial Acquirer

Traits:

- Reported growth depends heavily on acquisitions
- Adjusted metrics are prominent
- Goodwill and intangibles build over time
- Per-share value creation is the real test

Financial standard:

- Pass only if organic economics, integration costs, dilution, and retained-capital results are credible.

Valuation must respect:

- Do not give full credit to acquisition-adjusted numbers without per-share proof.

### Fragile / Promotional / Over-Leveraged

Traits:

- Reliance on refinancing, asset sales, aggressive adjustments, or narrative support
- Thin liquidity
- Weak owner earnings
- Management emphasis on presentation over economics

Financial standard:

- Usually Watchlist or Archive.

Valuation must respect:

- A low multiple does not repair weak economics or weak trust.

**Financial Pattern Result**: Asset-Light Compounder / Good Predictable / Asset-Heavy / Cyclical-Commodity / Financial-Insurer / Roll-Up / Fragile
