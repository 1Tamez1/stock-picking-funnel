# Stock Candidate Screening Questionnaire v5

Purpose: decide whether a stock deserves entry into **Business Underwriting**, belongs on a watchlist with a defined trigger, or should be archived.

This is the first stage of the funnel:

1. Screening
2. Business Underwriting
3. Management Underwriting
4. Financial Underwriting
5. Valuation and Position Size
6. Execution Rules

This document is only for **Screening**. It is not an underwriting memo, not a valuation memo, not a position-sizing memo, and not a buy decision. Its job is to reject weak ideas quickly, identify the few candidates worth deeper work, and create a clean handoff to the next funnel stage.

## Core Rules

- **Pass Screening** means "open Business Underwriting," not "buy."
- **Watchlist** means "potentially interesting, but a specific fact, price, or condition is missing."
- **Archive** means "stop unless the situation materially changes."
- Do not invent precision. Use rough ranges, evidence quality, and common sense.
- Unknowns are not neutral. A major unknown is a reason to Watchlist or Archive.
- Do not complete the whole form if a hard Archive condition appears early.
- A cheap price cannot fix bad people, bad accounting, a fragile balance sheet, or an unknowable business.
- A high-quality business is not interesting if the current price already requires excellent outcomes.
- Do not use a score. Scores create false comparability across unlike businesses.
- The final output must state the next action in the funnel.

Target depth:

- **Archive**: often 10-20 minutes is enough.
- **Watchlist**: usually 30-60 minutes.
- **Pass Screening**: enough work to justify Business Underwriting, not enough to underwrite the position.

# Evidence, Sources, And Confidence

Use evidence grades beside important claims.

- **F** = audited, reported, or directly filed fact
- **O** = observable operating, customer, competitive, or industry evidence
- **M** = management claim
- **I** = analyst inference
- **V** = item to verify later

Use confidence tags beside important judgments.

- **High** = supported by several reliable facts or a long relevant record
- **Medium** = plausible, but one important item needs more evidence
- **Low** = early read, thin evidence, or material uncertainty

Rule: if a screening decision depends mainly on **M**, **I**, or **V**, the stock normally belongs on the Watchlist, not Pass Screening.

Minimum source expectations for **Pass Screening**:

- Latest annual report or 10-K
- Latest quarterly report or 10-Q
- At least a quick proxy / compensation review
- Basic share count, debt, and cash flow history
- At least one relevant competitor, industry, or customer check when the moat claim matters

If these are not reviewed, Pass Screening requires an explicit reason.

# Part III. Hard Screening Gates

Each hard gate should result in **Pass**, **Watchlist**, **Archive**. A Watchlist result is acceptable only if the uncertainty is specific and verifiable.

## 1. Circle Of Competence And Understandability

Questions:

- Can the business be explained without jargon?
- Are the revenue drivers, cost drivers, customer decisions, and capital needs understandable?
- Are the key variables few enough to monitor?
- Is the value of the business tied to operating economics rather than a story, macro call, or technical claim?
- Are the important unknowns named honestly?

Rule:

- **Pass** if the business and value drivers are understandable at a screening level.
- **Watchlist** if one specific knowledge gap needs verification.
- **Archive** if the business is opaque, highly technical, or requires forecasts outside your competence.

**Result**: Pass / Watchlist / Archive

**Notes**:

## 2. Operating History And Evidence Base

Questions:

- Does the company have at least five years of meaningful operating history?
- Has it operated through a recession, competitive attack, industry downturn, or other stress period?
- Has it been profitable or owner-earnings positive in most recent years?
- Is the current business materially similar to the business that produced the historical record?
- If the record is short, is there strong observable evidence that the economics are already proven?

Rule:

- **Pass** if the record is long enough, relevant, and economically useful.
- **Watchlist** if the record is shorter but the economics are already observable and the missing evidence is specific.
- **Archive** if the thesis depends more on hope, pro forma claims, or a changed business than on evidence.

**Result**: Pass / Watchlist / Archive

**Notes**:

## 3. Business Quality And Moat Plausibility

Questions:

- Is demand durable, recurring, habitual, mission-critical, or otherwise likely to persist?
- Does the company have pricing power, cost advantage, switching costs, network effects, route density, distribution advantage, regulatory position, brand behavior, or another evidenced edge?
- Are returns on capital and margins consistent with the claimed advantage?
- Is the industry structure rational enough for good economics to persist?
- Is the moat widening, stable, narrowing, or unproven?
- What would a well-funded competitor do to attack the business?

Rule:

- **Pass** if there is plausible evidence of durable economics worth underwriting.
- **Watchlist** if quality may be real but requires one or two specific checks.
- **Archive** if the business is clearly commodity-like, structurally poor, or exposed to competitive destruction without a large and explicit special-situation discount.

**Result**: Pass / Watchlist / Archive

**Notes**:

## 4. No Turnaround Or Severe-Change Dependence

Questions:

- Does the idea require a new CEO, restructuring, product launch, macro recovery, cost cuts, financing event, or multiple rerating to work?
- Is the core business already good enough today?
- Would the idea still be interesting if the hoped-for improvement is late or partial?
- Is recent deterioration temporary and bounded, or structural?

Rule:

- **Pass** if the existing business is already acceptable.
- **Watchlist** if a good business has a specific, bounded, verifiable problem.
- **Archive** if the investment needs a turnaround to become investable.

**Result**: Pass / Watchlist / Archive

**Notes**:

## 5. Integrity, Accounting, And Incentive Kill Risk

This is not full Management Underwriting. It is a hard red-flag screen.

Questions:

- Any evidence of accounting games, aggressive revenue recognition, capitalization abuse, reserve releases, recurring "one-time" adjustments, or adjusted metrics that hide real costs?
- Does management discuss bad news plainly?
- Are incentives tied to per-share value creation, returns on capital, and long-term outcomes?
- Are stock-based compensation, options, related-party transactions, or share issuance likely to hurt owners?
- Would I be comfortable being a silent partner with this management team long enough to perform full underwriting?

Hard-fail items:

- Material accounting concern
- Promotional management
- Related-party abuse
- Chronic dilution without value creation
- Compensation divorced from owner returns
- Refusal to discuss obvious problems
- Repeated acquisitions that appear to increase size rather than per-share value

Rule:

- **Pass** if no material concern remains after a quick source review.
- **Watchlist** if the concern is narrow, nonfatal, and verifiable.
- **Archive** if integrity or accounting quality is materially suspect.

**Result**: Pass / Watchlist / Archive

**Notes**:

## 6. Balance Sheet And Permanent-Loss Risk

Questions:

- Can the company survive a recession, credit freeze, demand shock, or industry downturn?
- Are debt maturities, covenants, pensions, leases, legal liabilities, customer financing, or off-balance-sheet obligations material?
- Is leverage a tool, or is it the source of the return profile?
- For financials and insurers, are capital, liquidity, asset quality, reserves, underwriting discipline, and asset/liability matching understandable?

Rule:

- **Pass** if the balance sheet appears conservative for the business.
- **Watchlist** if leverage is probably manageable but needs a stress check in Financial Underwriting.
- **Archive** if debt, liquidity, hidden liabilities, asset quality, or leverage can create permanent impairment.

**Result**: Pass / Watchlist / Archive

**Notes**:

## 7. Valuation Knowability And Apparent Margin Of Safety

This is not the final Valuation and Position Size stage. It only asks whether the price is sane enough to justify further work.

Questions:

- Can normalized owner earnings, cash earnings, asset value, or liquidation value be approximated?
- Are current earnings normal, depressed, or peak-cycle?
- Can a rough value range be estimated without heroic growth, peak margins, or terminal multiple optimism?
- Is the current price at least potentially reasonable for the business quality?
- Is there an apparent margin of safety, or is the business so exceptional that a smaller apparent discount may be worth underwriting?
- What would make the rough value range wrong?

Rule:

- **Pass** if the stock is not obviously expensive on conservative screening economics and there is a plausible path to an adequate return.
- **Watchlist** if quality may be high but price, normalized economics, or one value driver needs verification.
- **Archive** if value is unknowable or the current price already requires excellent outcomes.

**Result**: Pass / Watchlist / Archive

**Notes**:

## 8. Opportunity Cost And Funnel Fit

Questions:

- Is this potentially better than cash, an index, and current portfolio alternatives?
- Is the idea good enough to spend scarce underwriting time on?
- Is the next stage clearly Business Underwriting, or is the real question management, financial leverage, valuation, catalyst timing, or execution?
- Is this a normal operating business, a financial/insurer, a special situation, or Too Hard?

Rule:

- **Pass** if the next step is a clearly defined Business Underwriting question.
- **Watchlist** if the business may be attractive but the trigger is price or one missing fact.
- **Archive** if the opportunity cost is weak or the next action is vague.

**Result**: Pass / Watchlist / Archive

# Part IX. Business Category And Next Underwriting Standard

Pick the category based on economics, not story appeal. Do not use a criteria count mechanically.

## Category Options

### Exceptional Compounder

Traits:

- Durable demand
- Evidenced moat
- Pricing power
- High returns on tangible capital
- Low capital intensity or high-return reinvestment runway
- Conservative balance sheet
- Owner-oriented management
- Long runway with limited obsolescence risk

Screening standard:

- Can pass with a smaller apparent discount only if durability, reinvestment, and valuation evidence are unusually strong.
- Watchlist if price is merely fair but quality is exceptional.

Business Underwriting must prove next:

- Why demand and competitive advantage can persist.
- Why incremental capital can be deployed at attractive returns.
- Why the moat is not already fully reflected in the current price.

### Good Predictable Business

Traits:

- Understandable economics
- Acceptable returns on capital
- Some moat or competitive edge
- Stable margins and owner earnings
- Sound balance sheet
- Reasonable management

Screening standard:

- Needs a visible discount or an unusually clear path to per-share value growth.

Business Underwriting must prove next:

- Why the business is predictable enough.
- Why economics are durable enough.
- Why the price compensates for not being exceptional.

### Cyclical / Commodity / Asset-Heavy Business

Traits:

- Earnings depend on commodity prices, macro cycles, capacity, rates, or asset values
- ROIC and margins are unstable
- Timing and balance sheet matter heavily
- Pricing power is weak or intermittent

Screening standard:

- Use normalized or trough-aware earnings.
- Require a strong balance sheet and a large discount.
- Archive if the thesis depends on peak earnings, favorable commodity prices, or leverage.

Business Underwriting must prove next:

- Where normalized economics are.
- Why supply/demand or industry structure is not structurally hostile.
- Why the company survives the downside case.

### Financial / Insurer

Traits:

- Value depends on underwriting, credit quality, capital, liquidity, reserves, asset/liability match, and management discipline.

Screening standard:

- Pass only if capital, reserving/credit quality, and management incentives are understandable enough to justify specialist underwriting.
- Archive if leverage, asset quality, or accounting makes value unknowable.

Next step:

- Use a financial/insurer-specific underwriting process if the normal business screen is insufficient.

### Special Situation

Traits:

- Catalyst, transaction, liquidation, spin-off, legal claim, restructuring, or event-driven return.

Screening standard:

- Use a separate special-situation checklist before underwriting.
- Do not force a special situation through a compounder screen.

Next step:

- Use a special-situation process.

### Too Hard

Traits:

- Opaque economics
- Too many moving parts
- Heavy dependence on unknowable technology, regulation, financing, or macro outcomes
- Value cannot be approximated

Screening standard:

- Archive.

**Business Category Result**: Exceptional Compounder / Good Predictable / Cyclical-Commodity / Financial-Insurer / Special Situation / Too Hard
